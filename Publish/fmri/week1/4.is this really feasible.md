## Three Reasons This Works

### 1. The BOLD Moments Dataset Was Built for Exactly This Kind of Project

The dataset isn't just *usable* for your RAG pipeline—it's **tailor-made** for it. Here's what the paper confirms :

| Requirement for RAG | How BMD Delivers |
|---------------------|------------------|
| **Paired fMRI + text** | 5 sentence descriptions + spoken transcriptions for every single video |
| **Large retrieval corpus** | 1,000 videos in training set → 1,000 distinct brain-text pairs to index |
| **Independent test set** | 102 held-out videos with 10 repetitions each → statistically robust evaluation |
| **Diverse semantic content** | Covers 305 scene labels, 1,002 object labels, 261 action labels |

The authors explicitly designed this dataset to enable "developing methodologies to model rapid event BOLD signals" and "bridging the gap between still image and longform movie perception" . Your RAG approach falls squarely into that first category.

### 2. The RAG-for-Brain-Decoding Idea Has Already Been Validated

This is crucial: **You're not guessing that retrieval helps—people have already shown it works.**

- **Brain-Aug (ACM MM 2024)** : Used fMRI signals to augment text queries and demonstrated "significantly improved document ranking accuracy, particularly for ambiguous queries." Their approach is the conceptual mirror of yours (brain→query enhancement vs. your brain→retrieval→generation).

- **PIRTA (arXiv 2024)** : A cross-modal RAG framework for 3D brain MRI that "mitigates the need for learning cross-modal mapping" by retrieving similar images with paired reports. This is *structurally identical* to what you're proposing—just on clinical MRI instead of fMRI.

The mechanism makes sense: retrieval turns closed-book decoding into open-book decoding. Instead of forcing the LLM to generate text purely from the fMRI embedding (which is lossy), you give it examples of what similar brain activity has produced before.

### 3. Your "Integration Only, No Training" Strategy is Smart

You're explicitly **not** trying to train fMRI encoders from scratch or fine-tune LLMs. This is a realistic scope for a Master's thesis:

| Task | Effort | Success Probability |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| Download BMD + set up environment | 1-2 days | Near 100% |
| Run pre-trained fMRI encoder (MindLLM/fMRI-LM) | 1 week | High (code is public) |
| Build FAISS index with text embeddings | 2-3 days | High |
| Implement retrieval + LLM prompting | 1 week | High |
| Run evaluations (BLEU/ROUGE/BERTScore) | 3-5 days | High |

The only real uncertainty is how well MindLLM or fMRI-LM generalize to BMD's specific stimulus structure. But that uncertainty is itself **interesting research results**—either it works (great) or it doesn't (you analyze why and propose fixes).

## What Could Go Wrong? (And Why It's Still Fine)

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|------------|------------|
| **Pre-trained fMRI encoders don't work well on BMD** | Moderate | Your baseline establishes this. Even negative results are publishable—they tell the field which encoders generalize. |
| **LLM hallucinations with retrieved context** | Low | Standard RAG problem with known solutions (better prompting, relevance filtering). |
| **Retrieval doesn't improve over baseline** | Possible | Still a valid result. Your thesis becomes "RAG doesn't help for this task, here's why"—that's science. |
| **BMD download is too large** | Low | The repository specifically recommends *not* downloading everything; you only need version B derivatives . |

**The one genuine constraint**: You need a GPU (RTX 3090/4090 or similar) and ~200-500GB storage. Without that, the project stalls. Confirm this first.
